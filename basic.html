Circle of fifth's importance

The Circle of Fifth is a visual tool to describe music theory, it's as important as the multiplication table in mathematics.

In the circle of fifths, moving clockwise along the circle, each key is a perfect fifth higher than the previous one.

In addition, you can also find the rules of sharps and flats or create chord progressions based on this circle. Such as royal road progression, canon progression and so on.

In fact, over hounds music theories can be found in the circle of fifths. It is a powerful tool for describing music theory.







Basic definations

Here I introduce some basic definitions of a circle, including circumference, diameter and radius. I believe everyone has learned them, so I will just review them here.

Circumference is the length of a circle. You can imagine a circle as a shape surrounded by a closed thin line, and the "length of the line" is the circumference of the circle.

Diameter is a line segment with both endpoints on the circumference and passing through the center of the circle. There are countless diameters in a circle.

Radius is half the length of the diameter. You can think of it as a line segment with the center of the circle as one endpoint and the other endpoint at any point on the circumference. Like the diameter, a circle also has infinitely many radii.





Chord and arc

The picture on the left is the part of the pop song "Never Gonna Give You Up" by Rick Astley, released on 27 July 1987, it was the best-selling song of that year. The chord of this part is G major and A major.

You can click the image to go to the reference website, play the song by the play bar or go to the youtube video by the text link.



After connect Gmajor and Amajor in the circle of fifths, we get a line and two curves.

This line is called chord, it definated as a line with both endpoints on a circle.

This curve is called arc, it's a part of the circumference.

A diameter is also a chord and it is the longest chord in a circle.


There are 3 different types of arcs, which are Minor arc, Semicircle and Major arc. Here is a table showing the relationship between them:

Term Length central angle degree
Minor arc <half of the circumference >180°
Semicircle =half of the circumference =180°
Major arc >half of the circumference >180°
